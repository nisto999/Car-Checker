Which car? Straight talk

  • Fiesta 1.25 (Mk7.5) — the sensible default: dry belt, cheap to insure, parts everywhere. Slower and thirstier than the EcoBoost, and that's fine for a first car.
  • Fiesta 1.0 EcoBoost / 1.1 — nicer to drive, but every one carries the wet-belt question. Only buy with oil-change evidence, and price the belt job in past 8 yrs / 100k.
  • Honda Jazz (manual or post-2011 CVT) — chain-driven, no belt anxiety, statistically the most reliable car here. The catches: manual Jazzes are scarce secondhand, they cost more for the same age — and insurance groups are surprisingly high (13–19, vs 2–9 for a base Fiesta or Fabia). Get a quote before assuming it's the cheap option. Avoid the 2009–11 i-SHIFT entirely.
  • Škoda Fabia 1.0 MPI (2014 on) — the Fabia equivalent of the Fiesta 1.25: slow, chain-driven, low insurance, solid. The 2014-on TSI engines use a dry belt — the notorious chain problem only applies to the older 2010–14 1.2 TSI.
  • Toyota Yaris petrol (1.0/1.33) — arguably the safest bet here: chain-driven, insurance groups 2–8, and Toyota's reliability is the real thing. Avoid the MultiMode automated auto; the hybrid is excellent but pricier and groups a bit higher. The Auris is the bigger sibling with the same virtues.
  • VW Polo / Golf — nicer cabins, but engine choice is everything. The 2014-on 1.0/1.4 TSI (EA211) and 1.0 MPI use a dry belt or are trouble-free; the older 1.2/1.4 TSI (EA111, pre-2014) carries the same chain-tensioner risk as the Fabia. Golf is a size up and insures higher; GTI/R are non-starters for a young driver on group and price.
  • Mini Hatch — desirable and fun, but the 2007–2013 R56 Prince engine has the 'death rattle' timing-chain fault that can scrap an engine — the cold-start listen is essential. The 2014-on F56 (BMW engines) is a big reliability step up. Either way, Minis insure higher and hold their price, so you pay more up front and more to cover it.
  • Value tier — Hyundai i20 / Kia Rio / Suzuki Swift / Mazda 2 / SEAT Ibiza — all proper superminis, not city cars. The non-turbo petrols (i20/Rio 1.2–1.4 MPi, Swift 1.2, Mazda2 1.5) are chain-driven, cheap to insure and genuinely low-drama; a used Kia/Hyundai may even have warranty left. Two catches: the Korean 1.0 T-GDi turbo (pre-2020) has its own chain-tensioner rattle, and the SEAT Ibiza is a Polo underneath — so the same EA111-vs-EA211 engine rule applies. Sport/FR/Cupra versions insure like hot hatches.
  • Gearboxes to avoid regardless of badge — Ford Powershift, Honda i-SHIFT, Toyota MultiMode (MMT), VW/Škoda DQ200 DSG. If the advert says automatic, check which one before viewing. (Toyota's hybrid eCVT is the exception — no clutch, very reliable.)
  • A score can't see everything — availability, drive feel and insurance quotes for the actual driver matter as much as the number. A 4/5 car you can insure beats a 5/5 car you can't.

Every car, every time

  • Run the free gov.uk MOT history check — look at mileage progression between tests, not just the current reading. Jumps backwards or long gaps are red flags.
  • HPI / history check (~£10) for outstanding finance, insurance write-off category, theft marker and plate changes.
  • Check outstanding recalls free at gov.uk vehicle recall checker.
  • V5C logbook: name and address match the seller, and the sale happens at that address. Never buy in a car park.
  • Cold start: arrive early and insist the engine is stone cold. A warmed-up engine hides rattles, smoke and poor starting.
  • Service history: stamps or invoices, correct oil grade, sensible intervals. A gap of 2+ years in servicing matters more than one high-mileage year.
  • Tyres: matching brands per axle, even wear. Uneven inner-edge wear suggests alignment or suspension issues.
  • Panel gaps and paint: mismatched shades or overspray on trim indicate accident repair.
